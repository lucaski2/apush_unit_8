APUSH · Unit 8

Mid-20th Century America

1945–1980 · Complete Study Guide

Unit Overview

Three interlocking threads hold Unit 8 together

1. Containment: Every foreign policy decision flows from the goal of stopping communism's spread — Korea, Vietnam, Guatemala, the Marshall Plan, détente, Camp David, SALT.
2. Rights Expansion: Each decade more Americans demand inclusion in the American promise — Black Americans, women, Latinos, Indigenous peoples, LGBTQ+ people. The Civil Rights Movement becomes a template.
3. Liberalism's Rise and Fall: FDR's New Deal → Truman → Ike (acceptance) → JFK → LBJ peak → Vietnam/backlash → Nixon → Carter malaise → Reagan waiting in the wings.

The Cold War (Foreign Policy)

U.S.–Soviet rivalry shaped every foreign policy decision for four decades. The core strategy was containment — don't let communism spread further. This led to the Marshall Plan, NATO, Korea, Vietnam, the Space Race, the Cuban Missile Crisis, détente, SALT treaties, and proxy wars across Asia, Latin America, and Africa.

The Civil Rights Era

The long struggle against American apartheid — segregation, disenfranchisement, economic exclusion. Fought through the courts (Brown), direct action (Montgomery, sit-ins, Selma), legislation (Civil Rights Act, Voting Rights Act), and ultimately Black Power when legal equality proved insufficient to address structural inequality.

Postwar Prosperity & Its Discontents

WWII created the middle class (GI Bill, union wages, booming economy). Suburbanization, baby boom, Sunbelt growth. But prosperity brought conformity and anxiety — nuclear fear, Red Scare, rigid gender roles. The Beat Generation, rock 'n' roll, and eventually the Counterculture pushed back.

LBJ's Great Society

The peak of American liberalism: Medicare, Medicaid, Voting Rights Act, War on Poverty, education funding, Immigration Act. LBJ had bigger congressional majorities than any president since FDR. But Vietnam consumed his presidency, resources, and political capital — then triggered a conservative backlash that defined American politics for the next 50 years.

Nixon Through Carter: Crisis & Disillusionment

Watergate destroyed trust in government. Stagflation destroyed economic confidence. Vietnam's fall destroyed confidence in American power. The Iranian hostage crisis destroyed confidence in Carter. By 1980, Americans had absorbed a decade of trauma — assassinations, defeat, corruption, inflation, humiliation — and were ready for Ronald Reagan's optimistic conservatism.

Identity Movements

The Civil Rights Movement became a template. Women's liberation (NOW, ERA, Roe), Chicano movement (UFW, Brown Berets), American Indian Movement (Wounded Knee), gay rights (Stonewall), Asian American movement — all demanded that America live up to its stated values. These movements reshaped culture, law, and politics in ways still felt today.

The Cold War

Why did the Cold War start?

WWII left only two superpowers standing. The U.S. and USSR had fundamentally incompatible systems — capitalist democracy vs. communist authoritarianism — and both had the military power and ideological conviction to shape the postwar world. Their wartime alliance collapsed almost immediately as Stalin installed puppet governments in Eastern Europe. The question was: how far would communism spread?

Foundational Policies (1947–49)

Containment (Kennan, 1946)

George Kennan's "Long Telegram" and "X Article": the USSR was inherently expansionist, but if contained long enough, it would eventually collapse from internal contradictions. Don't roll it back — just stop it from spreading. This became the foundation of all U.S. Cold War strategy.

Truman Doctrine (1947)

Greece and Turkey faced communist insurgencies. Truman asked Congress for $400M in aid, declaring the U.S. would "support free peoples" resisting communism. This was a massive break from isolationism — committing the U.S. to intervene anywhere communism threatened. Essentially applied containment globally.

Marshall Plan (1948)

Secretary of State George Marshall proposed $13.3 billion to rebuild war-devastated Western European economies. The logic: poverty breeds communism. Countries that received Marshall Plan aid included Britain, France, West Germany, Italy. The Soviets rejected it and pressured Eastern Europe to do the same. Hugely successful — Western Europe recovered rapidly.

NATO (1949)

North Atlantic Treaty Organization: 12 founding members agreed that an attack on one was an attack on all (Article 5). First permanent peacetime military alliance in U.S. history — Washington had warned against "entangling alliances." Triggered the Soviet creation of the Warsaw Pact (1955). NATO still exists today.

Berlin Blockade/Airlift (1948–49)

Stalin blocked all land routes to West Berlin (deep inside Soviet-controlled East Germany), hoping to starve it into submission. Truman refused to abandon it. The U.S. and allies flew in 2.3 million tons of supplies over 11 months — sometimes a plane landed every 90 seconds. Stalin backed down. First major Cold War standoff, U.S. won without firing a shot.

NSC-68 (1950)

Top-secret National Security Council report: argued the Soviet threat was existential, recommended quadrupling U.S. defense spending. Korean War's outbreak gave Truman political cover to implement it. Defense spending went from $13B to $50B. Set the pattern of massive military spending that defined the Cold War era.

Major Conflicts & Crises

1950–1953

Korean War

North Korea (backed by USSR and China) invaded South Korea June 25, 1950. Truman intervened under UN authorization without a congressional declaration of war — calling it a "police action." MacArthur pushed North Korean forces to the Chinese border, provoking China's entry with 300,000 troops. MacArthur publicly demanded attacking China; Truman fired him for insubordination, asserting civilian control of the military. Armistice signed July 1953 near the original border. Korea remains divided today at the 38th parallel. 36,000 Americans died — called "The Forgotten War."

1954

Guatemala (CIA Operation PBSUCCESS)

Democratically elected President Jacobo Árbenz nationalized land from United Fruit Company (a U.S. corporation). CIA organized a coup, replacing him with a military dictator. Pattern of U.S. interventionism in Latin America — prioritizing anti-communism over democracy. Set tone for decades of U.S. involvement in the region.

1953

Iran (Operation Ajax)

CIA and British intelligence overthrew Prime Minister Mossadegh, who had nationalized Iran's oil industry. Reinstalled the Shah. Created lasting anti-American resentment in Iran that exploded in the 1979 Islamic Revolution.

1957

Sputnik & the Space Race

The USSR launched the world's first satellite October 4, 1957. Americans were shocked — if Soviets could orbit a satellite, they could deliver nuclear warheads. Congress passed National Defense Education Act (1958), pouring money into science and math education. NASA created 1958. Kennedy committed to moon landing 1961. Apollo 11 landed July 20, 1969 — Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin. Enormous symbolic Cold War victory for the U.S.

1961

Bay of Pigs

CIA-trained Cuban exiles attempted to invade Cuba and overthrow Fidel Castro. JFK approved the plan but denied air support. Invasion was a fiasco — all 1,400 invaders captured. Major embarrassment for Kennedy. Strengthened Castro's position and pushed Cuba closer to USSR.

1962

Cuban Missile Crisis — 13 Days

U.S. U-2 spy plane photographed Soviet nuclear missile installations being built in Cuba. JFK was briefed October 16. Options: air strike (risked nuclear war), invasion (same), or naval blockade (less provocative). Kennedy chose "quarantine" (blockade) and demanded Soviets remove missiles. 13 days of terrifying standoff. Resolved: USSR removed missiles publicly; U.S. pledged not to invade Cuba and secretly removed missiles from Turkey (not announced for 25 years). Hotline established between White House and Kremlin. Closest humanity has come to nuclear war.

1965

Dominican Republic Intervention

LBJ sent 20,000 troops when a left-wing government was elected, fearing "another Cuba." Reflected deep anxiety about Latin American leftism during Cold War.

1964–1975

Vietnam War (see Vietnam tab for full detail)

Gulf of Tonkin Resolution 1964 gave LBJ blank check. Escalation to 543,000 troops. Tet Offensive 1968 shattered public confidence. Nixon's Vietnamization. Bombing of Cambodia (secret, then revealed). Paris Peace Accords 1973. Fall of Saigon April 30, 1975. 58,000 Americans killed; 2–3 million Vietnamese.

1973

Yom Kippur War & OPEC Embargo

Egypt and Syria attacked Israel on Yom Kippur. U.S. airlifted weapons to Israel; Israel prevailed. Arab OPEC nations imposed oil embargo on U.S. for its support of Israel. Gas prices quadrupled; long lines at gas stations. Revealed dangerous U.S. dependence on foreign oil. Contributed to stagflation and the energy crisis that plagued the 1970s.

1972–79

Détente

Nixon and National Security Advisor Henry Kissinger pursued "realist" foreign policy — engage with adversaries, reduce tensions, manage rivalry rather than crusade against it. Key achievements: SALT I (1972), Opening of China (1972), Helsinki Accords (1975). Controversial among conservatives who saw it as accepting communist gains. Ended when Soviets invaded Afghanistan (1979).

1978

Camp David Accords

Carter brokered 13-day negotiations between Israeli PM Begin and Egyptian President Sadat at Camp David. Framework for peace between Israel and Egypt — Egypt became first Arab nation to recognize Israel. Egypt got Sinai Peninsula back. Sadat assassinated 1981 for making peace. Carter's greatest foreign policy achievement.

Decolonization & the Non-Aligned World

As European empires collapsed after WWII, dozens of new nations formed in Asia, Africa, and the Middle East. Both superpowers competed to bring these nations into their orbit. The Non-Aligned Movement (led by India's Nehru, Egypt's Nasser, Yugoslavia's Tito) tried to remain independent. The U.S. repeatedly undermined this by supporting anti-communist authoritarian governments over nationalist/leftist democratic movements — sowing long-term resentment. Examples: Vietnam (supported corrupt South Vietnamese government), Guatemala (overthrew democratic Árbenz), Iran (overthrew democratic Mossadegh), Congo (supported Mobutu after Lumumba assassination).

The Cold War at Home

McCarthyism & The Second Red Scare

Origins of the Second Red Scare

Multiple shocks in 1949–50 triggered panic: China "fell" to communism (October 1949), Soviets tested their first atomic bomb (August 1949), and Alger Hiss (senior State Department official) was convicted of perjury related to Soviet espionage. The Communist Party USA had 75,000 members. Fear was real, even if the response was wildly disproportionate.

HUAC & Hollywood

House Un-American Activities Committee investigated communist influence in Hollywood. The "Hollywood Ten" refused to testify and were jailed for contempt of Congress. Hundreds of writers, directors, and actors were blacklisted. Films like "High Noon" (1952) were read as allegories for McCarthyism. Dalton Trumbo wrote under a pseudonym for years.

Senator Joseph McCarthy

Republican Senator from Wisconsin. February 1950 speech in Wheeling, WV: claimed to have a list of 205 communists in the State Department (he had no such list). Brilliant demagogue — constantly changed his numbers and accusations, always staying one step ahead. Targeted government officials, professors, union leaders. Used guilt by association, innuendo, and public pressure. His method: make the accusation, the damage is done whether proven or not.

Army-McCarthy Hearings (1954)

McCarthy investigated communist infiltration of the U.S. Army. Televised nationally — 20 million watched. Army lawyer Joseph Welch delivered the devastating line: "Have you no sense of decency, sir, at long last?" The hearings showed McCarthy as a bully and reckless liar. Senate voted 67-22 to censure him for "conduct unbecoming." He died in 1957, largely ignored, of liver disease (alcoholism).

Rosenbergs & Alger Hiss

Julius and Ethel Rosenberg convicted of passing nuclear secrets to Soviets. Executed 1953 — the only civilian executions for espionage in U.S. history. Alger Hiss, a State Department official, convicted of perjury related to Soviet espionage. Richard Nixon made his early career pursuing Hiss. Soviet archives later confirmed both cases had some basis, but the broader Red Scare swept up many innocents.

Legacy of McCarthyism

The Red Scare chilled free speech and political dissent for years. The term "McCarthyism" entered the language as a synonym for reckless accusation and guilt by association. It disproportionately targeted leftists, civil rights activists, LGBTQ people (the "Lavender Scare"), and immigrants. Also gave conservatives political cover to label any liberal reform as "communist."

Eisenhower & The Military-Industrial Complex

Eisenhower's Presidency (1953–61)

Five-star general who won WWII in Europe, then served two popular terms as president. "Modern Republicanism": accepted the New Deal's basic structure but didn't expand it. Balanced the budget. Ended the Korean War. Refused to escalate when Soviets crushed Hungarian uprising (1956). Sent troops to Little Rock to enforce school desegregation (1957) — reluctantly but importantly.

The Farewell Address Warning

January 17, 1961 — three days before JFK's inauguration. Eisenhower, who had spent his life in the military, warned: "In the councils of government, we must guard against the acquisition of unwarranted influence, whether sought or unsought, by the military-industrial complex. The potential for the disastrous rise of misplaced power exists and will persist." A general warning about war profiteering and the self-perpetuating logic of defense spending. Remarkably prescient.

"New Look" Defense Policy

Ike rejected massive conventional armies (too expensive) in favor of nuclear deterrence — "more bang for the buck." Relied on the threat of "massive retaliation" — any Soviet aggression might be met with nuclear response. This kept defense spending down but raised terrifying stakes if deterrence failed. CIA and covert operations also expanded as cheaper alternatives to conventional military action.

U-2 Spy Plane Incident (1960)

American spy plane shot down over Soviet territory. Ike initially lied about its mission, then was caught. Khrushchev used it to blow up the Paris Summit. Showed the risks of covert intelligence operations. Damaged Ike's final months in office and contributed to Cold War tensions JFK inherited.

The Anti-Vietnam War Movement

1964

Gulf of Tonkin Resolution

After (dubious) reports of North Vietnamese attacks on U.S. ships, Congress gave LBJ virtually unlimited authority to wage war in Vietnam. No formal declaration of war. Later revealed the second "attack" likely never happened. Passed 88-2 in Senate. This became the legal basis for the entire war.

1965–67

Campus Protests Begin

SDS (Students for a Democratic Society) led "teach-ins" at universities. March on Pentagon 1967 (100,000 protesters). Burning of draft cards. "Hey, hey, LBJ — how many kids did you kill today?" Draft disproportionately fell on poor and minority men who couldn't get deferments.

1968

Tet Offensive & Political Collapse

NVA and Viet Cong attacked 100+ South Vietnamese cities simultaneously during Tet (Lunar New Year). U.S. forces repelled the attack militarily, but it shattered the administration's claim that the U.S. was winning. Walter Cronkite declared the war a "stalemate." LBJ's approval collapsed. RFK entered the race. LBJ announced he would not run for re-election.

1968

Chicago Democratic National Convention

Chicago Mayor Daley's police beat antiwar protesters in the streets. The whole world watched on TV. Humphrey nominated despite not entering a single primary. The party fractured. Nixon won the election partly by promising a "secret plan" to end the war.

1970

Kent State

Nixon announced bombing of Cambodia — students protested across the country. Ohio National Guard opened fire on protesters at Kent State University, killing 4 students and wounding 9. 4 million students went on strike at 900 campuses. Jackson State (Mississippi) — police killed 2 Black students two weeks later, received far less coverage.

1971

Pentagon Papers

Daniel Ellsberg leaked a 7,000-page Defense Department study showing the government had systematically lied to Congress and the public about Vietnam. Nixon tried to suppress publication — Supreme Court ruled against him (New York Times Co. v. United States). Accelerated collapse of public trust in government.

1973

War Powers Act

Congress passed over Nixon's veto. Required president to notify Congress within 48 hours of committing troops to combat; must withdraw in 60 days without congressional approval. Response to both Vietnam and Nixon's secret bombing of Cambodia. Every president since has disputed its constitutionality but partially complied.

Postwar American Society

The Postwar Economic Boom

GI Bill (Servicemen's Readjustment Act, 1944)

Provided returning WWII veterans with college tuition (8 million used it), low-interest home loans (enabled suburbanization), unemployment insurance, and vocational training. Transformed American society — created a massive new middle class. Universities expanded enormously. Home ownership became the norm rather than the exception. Largely excluded Black veterans through discriminatory application by banks and colleges.

Baby Boom (1946–1964)

76 million babies born as returning veterans started families. Changed everything: schools built, suburbs expanded, consumer goods markets exploded. Boomers grew up in postwar prosperity, absorbed its contradictions, and became the generation that challenged everything in the 1960s and 70s. Their large numbers have dominated American politics ever since.

Levittowns & Suburbanization

William Levitt built mass-produced identical homes on Long Island (1947) — sold to veterans for $8,000 with no down payment. Suburbs exploded across America. Whites moved out; cities became majority-minority. Suburban life was car-dependent, conformist, and racially segregated by design (Levitt refused to sell to Black families; FHA redlining denied Black veterans equal access to loans). The suburb became the defining American living space.

Sunbelt Migration

Population shifted dramatically from the Rust Belt (Northeast/Midwest) to the Sunbelt (South and Southwest — Florida, Texas, Arizona, California). Enabled by: air conditioning, defense industry jobs (especially aerospace), lower cost of living, right-to-work laws. Political consequences: shifted electoral power from liberal Northern cities toward more conservative regions. California became the most populous state 1963. Texas and Florida emerged as major power centers.

Culture of Conformity (1950s)

The "Consensus" Culture

The 1950s celebrated sameness: identical ranch houses, identical cars, identical TV shows, identical gender roles. Sociologist David Riesman's "The Lonely Crowd" (1950) described Americans as "other-directed" — defining themselves by peer approval rather than inner values. William Whyte's "The Organization Man" (1956) criticized the conformity demanded by large corporations. Everyone was expected to be normal, and normal was narrowly defined.

Cold War Anxiety

Beneath the prosperity lay deep fear. The Soviets had the bomb. They might attack at any moment. Duck-and-cover drills in schools. Backyard fallout shelters. The 1950s nuclear family partly emerged from genuine fear — suburban homes away from city centers were survival strategy. The "Atomic Café" culture (a 1982 documentary assembled this era's propaganda) shows how thoroughly nuclear anxiety saturated daily life.

Gender Roles

The "feminine mystique" (Betty Friedan's term): middle-class white women were expected to find complete fulfillment as wives and mothers in the suburbs. Women who worked outside the home were seen as threats to family stability. The postwar period actually reversed WWII gains for women in the workforce. Meanwhile, "Rosie the Riveter" became a housewife again. This manufactured contentment is what Friedan's 1963 book would explode.

Television

By 1955, half of American homes had a TV. It homogenized American culture, created national consumer markets, and promoted idealized suburban family life (Father Knows Best, Leave It to Beaver, Ozzie and Harriet). But television also showed Americans civil rights violence, anti-war protests, and Vietnam — eventually turning opinion against both. TV transformed politics: JFK won partly because he looked better on TV than Nixon.

Challenges to Conformity

The Beat Generation

A small but culturally influential literary/artistic movement rejecting mainstream materialism. Allen Ginsberg's "Howl" (1956) — raw, explicit, and openly gay. Jack Kerouac's "On the Road" (1957) — celebrated spontaneity and freedom. William S. Burroughs. The "beatniks" lived in coffee shops in Greenwich Village and San Francisco. Direct precursor to the 1960s counterculture.

Rock 'n' Roll

Emerged from Black rhythm and blues. Chuck Berry, Little Richard, Fats Domino — crossed racial lines when white artists covered their songs (Elvis). Caused moral panic among white parents — its sexual energy, Black origins, and teenage rebellion threatened the social order. Dick Clark's American Bandstand helped mainstream it. Rock 'n' roll was genuinely subversive: it brought Black culture into white suburban homes and celebrated teenage autonomy.

Betty Friedan & The Feminine Mystique (1963)

Friedan surveyed her Smith College classmates (all middle-class white women) and found widespread depression, anxiety, and unfulfillment despite (or because of) their perfect suburban lives. She named "the problem that has no name" — the cultural expectation that women should find complete fulfillment in domesticity. The book sold 1.4 million copies in its first three years and directly helped launch second-wave feminism. Friedan co-founded NOW (National Organization for Women) in 1966.

The Counterculture (1960s)

The Baby Boom generation came of age skeptical of their parents' conformist, Cold War, and racist world. San Francisco's Haight-Ashbury became the center of hippie culture — communal living, psychedelics (LSD), free love, protest music (Bob Dylan, Joan Baez, Jefferson Airplane, Janis Joplin). Woodstock (August 1969): 400,000 attended. Linked to anti-Vietnam sentiment but also to broader rejection of corporate, consumerist, militaristic American culture. Faded by early 1970s — partly co-opted by commercial culture.

JFK (1961–1963)

The New Frontier

JFK was the first president born in the 20th century. His inaugural address ("Ask not what your country can do for you...") captured a generation. Created the Peace Corps (1961). Committed to moon landing. His youth and glamour ("Camelot") contrasted sharply with Eisenhower's grandfatherly image. Supported civil rights cautiously — didn't push Congress hard until Birmingham's images shocked the nation.

Bay of Pigs (April 1961)

CIA plan, inherited from Eisenhower: 1,400 CIA-trained Cuban exiles would land in Cuba and trigger a popular uprising against Castro. JFK approved but denied air support. The invasion was a catastrophic failure — all invaders captured or killed. JFK took full responsibility publicly. The fiasco emboldened Khrushchev, who thought he could push around this young, inexperienced president — contributing to the Berlin Wall and Cuban Missile Crisis.

Cuban Missile Crisis (October 1962)

Resolved successfully — see Cold War section for detail. JFK's finest hour: kept cool under pressure, resisted military advisors who wanted immediate air strikes, gave Khrushchev a face-saving way out. The resolution established JFK's credibility and showed that nuclear brinksmanship could be managed. Led to the Limited Nuclear Test Ban Treaty (1963) and the Moscow-Washington hotline.

Assassination (November 22, 1963)

Shot in Dallas motorcade. Lee Harvey Oswald arrested; killed by Jack Ruby two days later. LBJ sworn in on Air Force One. The Warren Commission concluded Oswald acted alone — conspiracy theories have persisted ever since. The assassination shattered the optimism of the early 1960s. LBJ used the national grief and JFK's legacy as political capital to push through the Civil Rights Act and Great Society legislation that Kennedy hadn't been able to pass.

Environmental Movement & Immigration

Origins of Environmentalism

Rachel Carson's "Silent Spring" (1962) documented how pesticides (especially DDT) were killing birds and entering the food chain — bestseller that launched the modern environmental movement. Santa Barbara oil spill (1969). Cuyahoga River in Cleveland caught fire (1969). First Earth Day: April 22, 1970 — 20 million Americans participated. Nixon (ironically) created the EPA (1970), signed the Clean Air Act, Clean Water Act, and Endangered Species Act. Represented a new understanding that economic growth had environmental costs.

Immigration Act of 1965 (Hart-Celler Act)

Abolished the national-origins quota system established by the Immigration Act of 1924 (which heavily favored Northern and Western Europeans). Replaced it with preferences for family reunification and skills. Sponsors expected only modest changes — they were wildly wrong. Immigration from Asia, Latin America, Africa, and the Caribbean exploded. By 2000, immigrants from these regions and their descendants were transforming American culture, politics, and demographics. One of the most consequential laws of the 20th century.

The Civil Rights Movement

WWII Origins (1941–1950)

World War II created the conditions for the Civil Rights Movement. Over one million Black Americans served in a segregated military fighting against fascism and racism abroad — then returned home to Jim Crow. The Double V Campaign (promoted by the Pittsburgh Courier newspaper): victory against fascism overseas AND victory against racism at home. Black workers migrated north for factory jobs (Great Migration), building political power in Northern cities. A. Philip Randolph threatened a march on Washington in 1941, forcing FDR to ban discrimination in defense industries. Truman, moved by reports of returning veterans being beaten by police, desegregated the armed forces by executive order in 1948. The NAACP's legal strategy under Thurgood Marshall was methodically dismantling "separate but equal" in courts throughout the 1940s, culminating in Brown.

Full Timeline

1954

Brown v. Board of Education

NAACP lawyer Thurgood Marshall argued before the Supreme Court that segregated schools were inherently unequal — psychological damage to Black children. Chief Justice Earl Warren wrote a unanimous opinion: "separate educational facilities are inherently unequal." Overturned Plessy v. Ferguson (1896). The South's response: "Southern Manifesto" signed by 101 congressional representatives vowing to resist. 10 years after Brown, only 2% of Southern Black children attended integrated schools. Brown was a legal victory that required immense political struggle to enforce.

1955–1956

Montgomery Bus Boycott

Rosa Parks arrested December 1, 1955 for refusing to give up her bus seat. The Women's Political Council had already planned a boycott — Parks' arrest was the spark. Black community organized and sustained a 381-day boycott of city buses — the economic backbone of the transit system. MLK, a 26-year-old new pastor at Dexter Avenue Baptist Church, emerged as leader. Legal challenge succeeded: Supreme Court ruled bus segregation unconstitutional. Proved that nonviolent economic pressure worked, and made MLK the movement's national face.

1957

Little Rock Nine

Nine Black students enrolled at all-white Central High School. Arkansas Governor Orval Faubus called in the National Guard to block them. Eisenhower federalized the National Guard and sent 101st Airborne paratroopers to escort the students. Powerful image: soldiers with rifles escorting children to school. Showed that integration required federal force against state resistance.

1957

Civil Rights Act of 1957

First civil rights legislation since Reconstruction — weak, but symbolic. Created Civil Rights Commission. Lyndon Johnson, as Senate Majority Leader, maneuvered it through Congress. Established pattern of LBJ as civil rights legislative strategist.

1960

Greensboro Sit-Ins & SNCC

February 1, 1960: Four Black college freshmen sat at Woolworth's lunch counter in Greensboro, NC and asked to be served. They were refused but stayed until closing. Within weeks, sit-ins spread to 54 cities in 9 states. Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee (SNCC) founded at Shaw University — youth-led, more radical than SCLC, willing to take greater personal risk. The sit-in tactic worked: Woolworth's integrated its lunch counters in July 1960.

1961

Freedom Rides

CORE and SNCC activists rode interstate buses through the South to test Supreme Court rulings banning segregation in interstate travel. In Anniston, Alabama, a bus was fire-bombed. In Birmingham, riders were beaten by KKK while police stood aside (FBI informants in the crowd). Attorney General RFK negotiated with Alabama governor for protection. Federal government ultimately issued regulations enforcing desegregation of interstate travel facilities.

1962

Ole Miss (Meredith)

James Meredith attempted to enroll at University of Mississippi. Governor Ross Barnett physically blocked him. JFK federalized the Mississippi National Guard. Night of riots left 2 dead and 375 injured. Meredith enrolled the next morning with 500 federal marshals. JFK reluctant to confront Southern Democrats who controlled Congress — pushed toward stronger action by events on the ground.

1963

Birmingham Campaign

MLK and SCLC chose Birmingham deliberately — "the most segregated city in America." Police Commissioner "Bull" Connor predictably responded with fire hoses and police dogs against peaceful protesters, including children. Images broadcast worldwide. JFK called it "a national disgrace" and sent a civil rights bill to Congress. MLK arrested — wrote "Letter from Birmingham Jail" to white moderate clergy who criticized the timing: "injustice anywhere is a threat to justice everywhere."

Aug. 1963

March on Washington

250,000 people — the largest demonstration in American history to that point. MLK's "I Have a Dream" speech at the Lincoln Memorial. Coalition of civil rights groups, labor unions, religious organizations. Remarkable discipline and organization. Political goal: pressure Congress to pass JFK's civil rights bill. JFK met with leaders afterward and privately told them it was impressive. Two months later JFK was dead; LBJ used the moment to push the bill.

Sept. 1963

16th Street Baptist Church Bombing

KKK bombed Black church in Birmingham, killing four young girls. Addie Mae Collins, Cynthia Wesley, Carole Robertson, and Carol Denise McNair, ages 11–14. Shocked the nation. Accelerated pressure for civil rights legislation. Perpetrators not convicted until decades later.

1964

Freedom Summer & Civil Rights Act

SNCC brought hundreds of mostly white Northern college students to Mississippi to register Black voters. Three workers — Chaney, Goodman, Schwerner — murdered by KKK. Their deaths got national attention (partly because two were white). Civil Rights Act signed July 2, 1964: banned discrimination in public accommodations and employment; created EEOC; Title VII prohibited employment discrimination including sex. LBJ reportedly told aide after signing: "We have lost the South for a generation."

1965

Selma & Voting Rights Act

SCLC and SNCC organized marches from Selma to Montgomery to demand voting rights. March 7 — "Bloody Sunday" — state troopers attacked 600 marchers on the Edmund Pettus Bridge with clubs and tear gas. Broadcast nationally. LBJ addressed joint session of Congress: "We shall overcome." Voting Rights Act signed August 6, 1965. Eliminated literacy tests, poll taxes, and grandfather clauses. Required federal approval for voting law changes in states with histories of discrimination (Section 5). Black voter registration in the South exploded.

1965–68

Urban Uprisings

Legal victories hadn't addressed poverty, housing segregation, police brutality in Northern cities. Watts (Los Angeles, 1965): 34 killed, $40M damage — triggered by traffic stop. Newark (1967): 26 killed. Detroit (1967): 43 killed, 7,000 arrested, LBJ sent federal troops. Kerner Commission Report (1968): blamed "white racism" for creating conditions of poverty and frustration. "Two societies, one Black, one white — separate and unequal." White backlash: many whites blamed the violence on the movement, not the conditions the movement was fighting.

1966–68

Black Power

After Selma, SNCC chair Stokely Carmichael demanded "Black Power" — self-determination, community control, Black pride. Rejected integration as a goal (why integrate into a burning house?). Black Panther Party for Self-Defense founded by Huey Newton and Bobby Seale in Oakland, 1966. Armed patrols monitored police. Free breakfast for children, health clinics, community organizing. FBI Director J. Edgar Hoover called them "the greatest threat to the internal security of the country." COINTELPRO program infiltrated, disrupted, and helped eliminate Black Panther leadership through illegal means. Debate erupted within the movement: was nonviolence still viable after 1965?

1968

MLK Assassination

Martin Luther King Jr. was in Memphis supporting striking sanitation workers when he was shot on the balcony of the Lorraine Motel, April 4, 1968. He had been shifting focus toward economic justice and had come out against Vietnam (1967) — both made him more enemies. Riots erupted in 100+ cities. Fair Housing Act passed six days later. James Earl Ray convicted of the murder. The movement lost its most powerful unifying voice at the moment it needed it most.

Organizations

NAACP (founded 1909)

National Association for the Advancement of Colored People. Legal strategy: systematically challenge segregation in courts. Thurgood Marshall's NAACP Legal Defense Fund won Brown. Oldest civil rights organization. More conservative, institutionalized approach — worked within the system. Over 500,000 members by 1960s.

SCLC (1957)

Southern Christian Leadership Conference. MLK's organization. Base in Black churches. Mass direct action, nonviolence rooted in Christian theology and Gandhi's methods. Key campaigns: Montgomery, Albany (failed), Birmingham (succeeded), Selma. Hierarchical — MLK was central. Funded partly by Northern liberals and labor unions.

SNCC (1960)

Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee. Youth-led, more democratic structure, willing to take more risks than SCLC. Sit-ins, Freedom Rides, Freedom Summer, voter registration in dangerous rural areas. Initially nonviolent; after 1965 under Stokely Carmichael shifted to Black Power and expelled white members. John Lewis (later Congressman) was early SNCC leader.

CORE (1942)

Congress of Racial Equality. Founded on Gandhian nonviolence. Organized 1947 Journey of Reconciliation (precursor to Freedom Rides), the 1961 Freedom Rides. Later became more militant under Floyd McKissick and Roy Innis, embracing Black Power and Black nationalism.

Black Panther Party (1966)

Founded Oakland. Armed self-defense against police brutality. Ten-Point Program demanded full employment, decent housing, Black exemption from military service, and end to police brutality. Community survival programs: free breakfasts for 10,000 children daily, free health clinics. Federal and local authorities systematically destroyed them through COINTELPRO. Fred Hampton (Chicago chairman, 21 years old) killed in his bed in an FBI-directed police raid in 1969.

Government Response

  • Executive: Truman (desegregated army 1948); Eisenhower (enforced Little Rock reluctantly); JFK (sent marshals to Ole Miss, proposed Civil Rights bill); LBJ (signed CRA 1964, VRA 1965, Fair Housing Act 1968)
  • Judicial: Brown v. Board (1954); Loving v. Virginia (1967, struck down anti-miscegenation laws)
  • Legislative: Civil Rights Act 1957 (weak); Civil Rights Act 1964 (landmark); Voting Rights Act 1965; Fair Housing Act 1968

Spread of the Civil Rights Template

Chicano Movement

César Chávez and Dolores Huerta co-founded the United Farm Workers (UFW) — organized exploited agricultural laborers in California. Grape boycott (1965–70) was a national movement. "¡Sí Se Puede!" — "Yes We Can!" Reies López Tijerina led the Alianza Federal de Mercedes, demanding return of land stolen under Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo. Rodolfo "Corky" Gonzales founded the Crusade for Justice in Denver. La Raza Unida (José Ángel Gutiérrez) formed a third political party in South Texas. High school students in LA organized "blowouts" (walkouts) in 1968, demanding better conditions and Chicano teachers.

Women's Liberation Movement

National Organization for Women (NOW) founded 1966 by Betty Friedan and others. Lobbied for Equal Rights Amendment, equal pay, reproductive rights. ERA passed Congress 1972 — ratified by 35 of needed 38 states; Phyllis Schlafly's STOP ERA campaign blocked it. Title IX (1972): banned sex discrimination in education programs receiving federal funds — transformed women's sports. Roe v. Wade (1973): legalized abortion nationwide. Ms. Magazine founded by Gloria Steinem 1972. Debate between liberal feminism (equal rights within system) and radical feminism (patriarchy must be dismantled).

American Indian Movement (AIM)

Founded Minneapolis 1968 by Dennis Banks and Clyde Bellecourt, initially to respond to police brutality against urban Indians. "Red Power." Trail of Broken Treaties (1972): caravan to Washington, occupied BIA building. Wounded Knee Occupation (1973): 200 AIM members occupied Wounded Knee, SD (site of 1890 massacre) for 71 days, demanding treaty rights, investigation of corruption. Standoff with federal marshals. Demanded U.S. honor hundreds of broken treaties. FBI surveillance and prosecutions targeted AIM leadership.

Gay Rights Movement (Stonewall)

June 27–28, 1969: New York City police raided the Stonewall Inn (a gay bar in Greenwich Village). Patrons — including transgender women of color like Marsha P. Johnson and Sylvia Rivera — fought back. Spontaneous riots lasted several nights. Within months, Gay Liberation Front formed. Annual Gay Pride marches began on the anniversary. Before Stonewall, being gay was criminalized, pathologized (listed as mental disorder until 1973), and grounds for firing. Stonewall transformed a stigmatized population into a rights movement.

LBJ & The Great Society

The Peak of American Liberalism

Lyndon Baines Johnson was a master legislative politician who had spent his career in Congress. After Kennedy's assassination, he used national grief and JFK's legacy — and his own extraordinary skills — to pass more legislation than any president since FDR. The 1964 election gave him a supermajority in Congress. But Vietnam consumed everything — and the conservative backlash he feared ("We have lost the South for a generation") came even faster than he expected.

The Great Society Programs

Civil Rights Act (1964)

Banned discrimination in public accommodations (hotels, restaurants, theaters) and employment on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, or national origin. Created the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) to enforce employment provisions. "Sex" was added as a protected category by conservative Rep. Howard Smith — he thought it would kill the bill by making it ridiculous; instead it became a foundation for women's rights. LBJ used everything — personal pressure, parliamentary maneuver, JFK's memory — to overcome Southern Democratic filibuster (57 days).

Voting Rights Act (1965)

After Bloody Sunday at Selma, LBJ addressed Congress and used the movement's own words: "We shall overcome." The act outlawed literacy tests and poll taxes. Section 5 required states with histories of discrimination to get federal "preclearance" before changing voting laws. Federal registrars sent to Southern counties. Result: Black voter registration in Mississippi went from 6.7% to 59.8% within four years. One of the most effective laws in American history. Section 5 gutted by Supreme Court in Shelby County v. Holder (2013).

Medicare & Medicaid (1965)

Medicare: federal health insurance for Americans 65+. Medicaid: federal-state program for low-income Americans. Both signed by LBJ at Independence, Missouri with Harry Truman — who had tried and failed to pass universal health care in 1948 — watching. Largest expansion of the welfare state since Social Security. Fiercely opposed by the American Medical Association, which called it "socialized medicine." Today they cover 140+ million Americans.

Elementary & Secondary Education Act (1965)

First major federal aid to public K-12 education. Title I: funding for schools with high numbers of low-income students. Broke through decades of opposition from those who feared federal control of education or opposed aid to parochial schools. Reauthorized repeatedly — as No Child Left Behind (2001), then Every Student Succeeds Act (2015).

War on Poverty

Economic Opportunity Act (1964): Office of Economic Opportunity administered Head Start (early childhood education), Job Corps (job training for disadvantaged youth), VISTA (domestic Peace Corps), Community Action Programs (funded local antipoverty organizations). Controversial: Community Action Programs gave money to grassroots organizations that sometimes challenged local political machines — including Democratic ones. Critics from the right said it created dependency; critics from the left said it was inadequate and diverted by Vietnam.

Other Great Society Laws

  • Immigration Act (1965) — abolished national-origins quotas
  • Voting Rights Act extensions (1970, 1975, 1982)
  • Fair Housing Act (1968) — banned housing discrimination
  • Higher Education Act (1965) — created student loans/grants
  • National Endowments for Arts & Humanities (1965)
  • Public Broadcasting Act (1967) — created PBS and NPR
  • Consumer protection laws, auto safety standards (Nader)

1960s Elections

Election of 1964: LBJ vs. Goldwater

Senator Barry Goldwater of Arizona ran as an unabashed conservative — voted against Civil Rights Act on states' rights grounds, suggested the U.S. should consider using tactical nuclear weapons in Vietnam. LBJ's "Daisy Ad" showed a girl counting petals, then a nuclear explosion — ran only once but was endlessly replayed. LBJ won 61.1% — the largest popular vote margin in history. Democrats gained 37 House seats and 2 Senate seats. The Goldwater campaign, though crushed, mobilized young conservative activists (including future Reagan operatives) and seeded the conservative movement. The South began voting Republican.

Election of 1968: The End of Liberal Dominance

MLK assassinated April 4. RFK assassinated June 5 — shot moments after winning the California primary. Chicago DNC: Mayor Daley's police beat antiwar protesters in the streets, broadcast on live TV. Humphrey nominated without entering a single primary. George Wallace (American Independent Party, segregationist): won 46 Electoral votes, 13.5%. Nixon ran on "law and order" and a "secret plan" to end Vietnam. Nixon won 43.4% to Humphrey's 42.7% — a three-way race determined by Wallace pulling from Democrats. Beginning of the end of the New Deal coalition.

Vietnam Destroys the Great Society

The "Guns and Butter" Trap

LBJ tried to fund both the Great Society and the Vietnam War without raising taxes — fearing that a tax increase would cost him political support needed for domestic programs. The result was inflation. By 1968, the War on Poverty was being defunded to pay for Vietnam. The moral contradiction was even worse: LBJ was sending disproportionately Black and poor soldiers to die in Vietnam while telling them their country was working to address racial and economic injustice at home. MLK called the U.S. government "the greatest purveyor of violence in the world today" in his 1967 speech against the war. Tet Offensive (January 1968) proved that LBJ's optimistic war reports were lies. He chose not to run for re-election March 31, 1968.

Conservative Backlash & Increasing Partisanship

Religious Conservatives

The Great Society's cultural permissiveness — toleration of birth control, civil rights for unmarried women, funding for sex education — mobilized evangelical Christians. Jerry Falwell Sr. founded the Moral Majority 1979. Pat Robertson's 700 Club. Roe v. Wade (1973) became the galvanizing issue. The religious right became the backbone of the Republican Party.

The "Southern Strategy"

Nixon and Republican strategist Kevin Phillips calculated that white Southern voters — traditionally Democratic since the Civil War — were now available to Republicans. They had abandoned the Democratic Party over civil rights. Nixon's appeal to "law and order" and opposition to busing for integration attracted them. By 1980, the solid Democratic South had become the solid Republican South — the most dramatic political realignment in modern American history.

Left Critics

The New Left critiqued the Great Society as tinkering at the edges of a fundamentally unjust system. The War on Poverty addressed symptoms, not causes (structural racism, capitalism's inequality). Vietnam revealed that the liberal state was not fundamentally different from conservatism when it came to imperialism and militarism. The Port Huron Statement (SDS, 1962) had already articulated this critique before the Great Society existed.

Nixon, Ford & Carter (1969–1981)

Nixon's Presidency (1969–1974)

Southern Strategy & Silent Majority

Nixon strategist Kevin Phillips wrote "The Emerging Republican Majority" (1969) — detailed how to build a GOP coalition from white Southerners, ethnic Catholics, and suburban whites alienated by Democratic support for civil rights and counterculture. Nixon's "Silent Majority" speech (November 1969) appealed to Americans who hadn't protested — the working-class and middle-class whites who resented the counterculture and antiwar protesters. "Law and order" coded racial resentment without explicit racism.

Domestic Policy (Surprisingly Moderate)

Nixon was more moderate domestically than his rhetoric suggested. Created EPA (1970). Signed Clean Air Act, Water Act, Endangered Species Act. Established OSHA. Proposed (but failed to pass) a guaranteed annual income (Family Assistance Plan). Imposed wage and price controls during stagflation. Ended the gold standard 1971 ("Nixon shock"). Started affirmative action in federal contracting. New Federalism: revenue sharing gave block grants to states — reduced federal categorical control.

Opening of China (1972)

Nixon and National Security Advisor Henry Kissinger secretly prepared a rapprochement with the People's Republic of China — which the U.S. had refused to recognize since 1949. Nixon visited Beijing February 1972, met with Mao Zedong. Strategic logic: create a wedge between China and USSR, make the Soviet Union more worried about its eastern flank and thus more cooperative with the U.S. Ended 23-year isolation. One of the great diplomatic achievements of the Cold War — made possible precisely because Nixon was an anti-communist who couldn't be accused of being soft.

Détente & SALT I (1972)

Kissinger's "realist" approach: manage the Cold War, accept permanent coexistence with communism, use trade and diplomacy to reduce tensions. SALT I (Strategic Arms Limitation Treaty, May 1972): first agreement to limit nuclear weapons. Froze numbers of ICBMs and submarine-launched ballistic missiles. Also negotiated the Anti-Ballistic Missile Treaty (limiting missile defense systems). Helsinki Accords (1975, signed by Ford): recognized postwar European borders; USSR agreed to respect human rights (which it immediately violated but gave dissidents a legal basis to demand).

Vietnam: Vietnamization to Fall of Saigon

Nixon promised "peace with honor." Vietnamization: withdraw U.S. troops while building up South Vietnamese forces. Troop levels fell from 543,000 to 50,000. Meanwhile, secretly expanded the war: bombing of Cambodia (1969–73), Laos. When leaks revealed the Cambodia bombing, Congress was furious. Invasion of Cambodia (April 1970) triggered Kent State. Paris Peace Accords (January 1973): ceasefire, U.S. troops withdraw, POWs released. Congress cut off funding for continued bombing. South Vietnam fell April 30, 1975 — after Nixon resigned. U.S. helicopters evacuating from rooftops became iconic image of American failure.

Watergate (1972–1974)

The Scandal That Ended a Presidency — Full Account

June 17, 1972

The Break-In

Five men arrested breaking into Democratic National Committee headquarters at the Watergate hotel. Connected to Nixon's re-election campaign (CREEP — Committee to Re-Elect the President). Nixon's team paid $450,000 in hush money to the burglars.

1972–73

Cover-Up Unravels

Nixon won 1972 in a 49-state landslide (vs. McGovern). Washington Post reporters Woodward and Bernstein, guided by "Deep Throat" (FBI Deputy Director Mark Felt), traced the money. Senate Watergate Committee televised hearings riveted the nation. White House aide John Dean "turned" and testified Nixon directed the cover-up.

July 1973

The Tapes Revealed

Alexander Butterfield revealed that Nixon secretly taped all White House conversations. Special Prosecutor Archibald Cox subpoenaed the tapes. Nixon ordered Attorney General Elliot Richardson to fire Cox. Richardson resigned rather than comply. Deputy AG William Ruckelshaus also resigned. Solicitor General Robert Bork finally fired Cox — "Saturday Night Massacre." Public outrage enormous. Impeachment proceedings began.

July 1974

Supreme Court & Impeachment Votes

Supreme Court ruled 8-0 (including Nixon appointees): Nixon must turn over tapes (United States v. Nixon). House Judiciary Committee approved three articles of impeachment: obstruction of justice, abuse of power, contempt of Congress. "Smoking gun" tape showed Nixon ordered cover-up six days after the break-in.

August 9, 1974

Resignation

Nixon resigned — the only president to do so. Said farewell to White House staff: "Never be petty... always remember others may hate you but those who hate you don't win unless you hate them." Ford sworn in: "Our long national nightmare is over."

September 1974

The Pardon & Legacy

Ford pardoned Nixon for any crimes he might have committed as president — before any charges were filed. Public outrage cost Ford the 1976 election. Legacy of Watergate: collapse of trust in government. FISA Court created (1978). Campaign Finance Reform (FECA). Freedom of Information Act strengthened. The "-gate" suffix entered English for any political scandal.

Economic Crisis of the 1970s

End of Bretton Woods (1971)

The Bretton Woods system (1944): all currencies pegged to the dollar; the dollar convertible to gold at $35/ounce. Worked fine when the U.S. dominated the world economy. By 1971, inflation (from Vietnam spending), trade deficits, and European economic recovery had overloaded the system. Nixon unilaterally ended dollar-gold convertibility — "Nixon Shock." Led to floating exchange rates, global currency volatility, and contributed to inflation.

Stagflation

Standard economic theory said inflation and unemployment moved in opposite directions (the Phillips Curve). The 1970s broke the model: high inflation AND high unemployment simultaneously. Causes: oil price shocks (OPEC 1973 and 1979), deficit spending from Vietnam and Great Society, end of gold standard. Neither increasing spending (to fight unemployment) nor raising interest rates (to fight inflation) worked cleanly without worsening the other. Carter appointed Paul Volcker as Fed Chair in 1979, who began raising interest rates sharply — eventually ending inflation but causing severe recession in 1981-82.

OPEC Oil Crisis (1973 & 1979)

1973: Arab OPEC nations embargoed oil to U.S. for supporting Israel in Yom Kippur War. Oil prices quadrupled overnight. Gas lines stretched for miles. "Odd-even" rationing by license plate. National speed limit lowered to 55 mph. Revealed dangerous dependence on foreign energy. 1979: Iranian Revolution disrupted Iranian oil production, causing second oil shock. Carter put solar panels on the White House (Reagan removed them). Both crises fueled inflation and recession and profoundly shaped 1970s politics.

Ford (1974–1977) & Carter (1977–1981)

Gerald Ford: Healing or Betrayal?

Never won a national election — appointed VP under 25th Amendment after Spiro Agnew resigned (tax evasion), then became president when Nixon resigned. Decent man in an impossible position. Pardon of Nixon: granted "full, free, and absolute pardon" — meant to allow the country to move on; instead seen as a deal. WIN (Whip Inflation Now) buttons mocked as insufficient response to inflation. Oversaw Fall of Saigon. Lost 1976 to Carter by narrow margin. Survived two assassination attempts.

Jimmy Carter: The Unlucky President

Georgia governor, peanut farmer, Naval Academy graduate, born-again Christian. Outsider who ran against Washington corruption. Genuine intellect and integrity; poor politician who couldn't work with Congress. Successes: Camp David Accords (Begin and Sadat — Nobel Peace Prize for both). Panama Canal Treaty (returned canal, reduced Latin American resentment). Created Departments of Energy and Education. National energy policy. Failures: Stagflation. Iranian hostage crisis. Soviet invasion of Afghanistan. Grain embargo (hurt American farmers). "Malaise speech" (actually called "A Crisis of Confidence") — accurately diagnosed American spiritual crisis but was seen as blaming the public. Lost to Reagan in 49-state near-landslide.

Iranian Hostage Crisis (1979–1981)

The Shah of Iran (installed by CIA coup in 1953) fled the country in January 1979 as the Islamic Revolution swept Iran under Ayatollah Khomeini. Carter allowed the Shah into the U.S. for cancer treatment. November 4, 1979: Iranian students stormed the U.S. Embassy in Tehran and took 52 Americans hostage. Held for 444 days. Carter's response: froze Iranian assets, economic sanctions, diplomatic pressure — all failed. Rescue mission (Operation Eagle Claw, April 1980): catastrophic failure — helicopters malfunctioned in the desert, 8 soldiers died in an accident before reaching Tehran. Secretary of State Cyrus Vance resigned in protest. The hostages were released minutes after Ronald Reagan was inaugurated on January 20, 1981 — humiliating Carter one final time. Some historians suspect the Reagan campaign negotiated a deal to delay release ("October Surprise" — unproven but debated).

Continuing Civil Rights Issues (1970s)

Busing & White Backlash

Federal courts ordered busing of students to integrate schools that remained segregated due to residential patterns. Northern cities exploded — Boston's 1974-75 busing crisis was particularly violent. White flight accelerated. Nixon opposed busing politically while courts enforced it. The backlash to busing revealed that many Northern whites who had supported Southern civil rights were unwilling to integrate their own schools and neighborhoods. "Not in my backyard" — racism was not just Southern.

Affirmative Action Debates

Regents of University of California v. Bakke (1978): Supreme Court ruled strict racial quotas in university admissions were unconstitutional, but race could be considered as one factor among many. Steelworkers v. Weber (1979): upheld voluntary affirmative action in private employment. These debates crystallized the "reverse discrimination" argument that became central to the conservative coalition.
